My Experience with Programming Fundamentals

Category: Programming | Date: June 2026

In our very first semester of computer engineering, Programming Fundamentals (PF) was our core introduction to the magnificent world of coding. Writing the first lines of code and seeing the words "Hello World" printed on the dark terminal screen felt incredibly powerful and exciting. Learning about basic computational logic, variables, data types, loops, and conditions opened up a completely new way of looking at mathematical puzzles and logical problem-solving. It was fascinating to see how simple statements can control the behavior of a machine and make it perform complex calculations within milliseconds. It felt like learning a brand new language from scratch.

At the beginning, syntax errors, mismatched brackets, and missing semicolons were highly frustrating and exhausting. I used to spend hours trying to understand why my program was crashing on execution. But slowly and steadily, I learned that coding is much more about core logic building rather than just typing random lines of software commands. Spending late nights debugging a single nested loop taught me how computer programs think, read commands line by line, and execute tasks step by step. It required me to change my normal thinking habits and become highly detail-oriented because computers do exactly what you tell them to do, not what you want them to do.

The practical lab sessions of Programming Fundamentals were the most exciting part of my weekly academic schedule. Sitting in front of the desktop computer, receiving a blank problem sheet from the instructor, and trying to convert that human problem into a working algorithm was a true test of our minds. My classmates and I used to compare our logic flowcharts and check whose code was running with the least amount of lines. Those labs built a strong spirit of engineering teamwork and healthy competition among us, which made the difficult topics much more enjoyable.

As we moved towards more advanced topics like functions and structure parameters, the assignments became quite complex. I started maintaining a dedicated notebook where I would sketch out the logic flow manually using pencils before typing it on the code compiler. This strategy worked wonders for me because it cleared the structural confusion from my mind first. It taught me that a good engineer spends 70 percent of their time planning the logic and only 30 percent typing the actual code on the computer system.

Looking back, Programming Fundamentals was the real pillar that transformed my mindset from a passive consumer of software applications into an active creator of logical code. It gave me the foundational confidence that I can control digital devices and build useful programs through structured logic. This course was hard, and it took a lot of my hostel study hours, but the satisfaction of seeing a clean, error-free output after a long struggle was entirely worth it. It laid down the perfect foundation for my upcoming advanced data structures and machine learning implementations.
